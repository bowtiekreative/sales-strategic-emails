# Chris Voss Negotiation

Based on Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* — FBI hostage negotiation techniques for high-stakes sales, deals, and conflict resolution.

> **Core mantra:** "No" is a decision; "Yes" is a commitment. You want "No" early and often. The goal is "That's right" — not "You're right."

---

## 🔑 Core Principles

### 1. Tactical Empathy
Understanding the other side's feelings and perspective **without agreeing** with them.
- **Mirroring:** Repeat the last 1-3 words the other person said (with an upward inflection)
- **Labeling:** Name the emotion they're feeling: *"It sounds like you're frustrated with the timeline"*
- **Accusation Audit:** Get all their potential objections on the table first: *"You're probably thinking the price is too high, the timeline is too long, and we haven't proven ourselves yet"*

### 2. The Power of "No"
- "No" feels safe. "Yes" feels like a trap.
- Ask questions that invite "no": *"Is this a bad time to talk about this?"*
- "No" preserves autonomy — people fight harder for what they said no to than yes to

### 3. Calibrated Questions
Questions that start with **How** or **What** — never "Why" (which sounds accusatory).
- *"How am I supposed to do that?"* (forces them to solve their own objection)
- *"What makes you say that?"* (reveals their real objection)
- *"What would need to be true for this to work?"* (opens possibility)
- *"How can we make this work for both of us?"* (partnership framing)

### 4. The Ackerman Model (Bargaining)
A 6-step system for negotiating price:
1. **Set your target price** (your real goal)
2. **Offer 65% of target** (lowball but credible)
3. **Three raises** — 85%, 95%, 100% — each with decreasing increments
4. **Use odd numbers** ($49,823 not $50,000 — feels calculated not rounded)
5. **At the final number, throw in a non-monetary concession** (something they value that costs you nothing)
6. **Be increasingly reluctant** — each concession should sound painful

### 5. "That's Right" vs "You're Right"
- *"You're right"* = I'm exhausted, here's your cookie — no commitment follows
- *"That's right"* = You truly understand my perspective — this creates buy-in
- **How to get "That's right":** Summarize their situation so accurately they can't help but agree

### 6. The Late-Night FM DJ Voice
A calm, slow, downward-inflecting tone. Creates authority and disarms tension. Drop your voice at the end of sentences.

## 📝 Sales Email Templates

### Cold Outreach — Mirroring Opener
```
Subject: [Their company name] + [Your value prop]

Hi [Name],

I noticed [something specific about their business].

[Three-word mirror of their public challenge OR label of their likely feeling]

Most conversations about [topic] start with features. Ours should start with [specific outcome they want].

Are you open to a 15-minute conversation about [specific approach]?

Best,
[Your name]
```

### Handling Price Objection — Calibrated Question
```
I hear you on the budget. Let me ask you:

What would this have to deliver for it to make sense at the current price?

And how does that compare to what you're spending on [alternative/current solution]?

If I can show you how it pays for itself in [timeframe], would you be open to reconsidering?
```

### The "No" Opener (for re-engagement)
```
Subject: Quick question

Hi [Name],

Is this a bad time to follow up?

I know you're busy, so — would it make sense to put this on hold for now?

[No answer = polite permission to continue]
```

## ⚠️ Pitfalls

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---------|-------------|
| Asking "Why?" | Triggers defensiveness — people justify their position instead of opening up |
| Splitting the difference | Leaves both sides unsatisfied; signals you don't know your value |
| Accepting first "yes" | It likely means "yes to get rid of you" — not genuine agreement |
| Emotional reactivity | If you're triggered, you lose leverage. Use the DJ voice |
| Over-explaining | Weakens your position. Silence is powerful — let them fill it |

## 🎯 When to Use This Skill

**Trigger conditions:**
- Drafting a sales email or proposal
- Preparing for a negotiation call
- Handling client objections or pushback
- Discussing price or budget
- Writing a counter-proposal
- Conflict resolution with a client or partner

**Do NOT use when:**
- The relationship is purely transactional and commoditized
- Speed of close matters more than relationship
- The other side has no emotional investment
