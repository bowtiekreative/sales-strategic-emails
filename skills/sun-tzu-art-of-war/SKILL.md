# Sun Tzu — Art of War

Translated into sales and business strategy. The Art of War is about **winning before the battle begins** — positioning, intelligence, and knowing when not to fight.

> **Core mantra:** "Supreme excellence consists of breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."

---

## 🔑 Core Principles

### 1. Know Yourself, Know Your Enemy
> *"If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles."*

- **In sales:** Research the prospect's industry, their competitors, their pain points, and your own BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)
- **Before any proposal:** Map their stakeholders, budget constraints, decision timeline, and competing priorities

### 2. Winning Without Fighting
> *"The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting."*

- **In sales:** Position your solution so the competitor's option looks **riskier than doing nothing**
- Frame your offer as the path of least resistance, not as an attack on their current provider
- Let the prospect convince themselves — plant seeds, don't hammer arguments

### 3. Position Yourself Where Resistance Is Weakest
> *"Attack him where he is unprepared, appear where you are not expected."*

- **In sales:** Find the gap in the competitor's offering that your solution fills uniquely
- Target accounts where competitors have weak relationships or poor service reputations
- Enter the conversation where you add unique value — don't compete on price or features

### 4. The Five Essential Traits (for organizations)
1. **Moral influence** — Why do you do what you do? Purpose and values
2. **Climate** — Market conditions, timing, trends
3. **Terrain** — Your market position, channels, distribution
4. **Command** — Leadership, talent, decision-making
5. **Doctrine** — Systems, processes, repeatable success

### 5. Use Speed, Not Prolonged Warfare
> *"There has never been a protracted war from which a country has benefited."*

- **In sales:** Long sales cycles benefit the incumbent. Create urgency (but authentic urgency)
- Front-load value — give them a reason to move quickly
- Ask: *"What happens if we don't solve this in the next [timeframe]?"*

### 6. The 32 Strategic Principles (Sales Cheat Sheet)

| Principle | Sales Application |
|-----------|------------------|
| Appear weak when strong | Undersell your hand; let results speak |
| Appear strong when weak | Use social proof and case studies to signal credibility |
| Pretend inferiority | Let the prospect feel smarter — they'll lower their guard |
| Lure them with gain | Lead with value, ROI, and what they'll win |
| Pretend disorder | Be vulnerable — "We're still figuring this out" builds trust |
| Attack when unprepared | Follow up immediately after they mention a pain point |
| Avoid strength | Don't attack competitor features you can't beat |
| Wear them down | Consistent, value-driven nurture sequences |

### 7. Terrain Strategy for Deal Stages

| Terrain Type | In Sales | Strategy |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| **Accessible** | Warm lead, clear need | Move fast, direct pitch |
| **Entangling** | Complex deal, many stakeholders | Patient, build multiple relationships |
| **Temporary** | Short window, limited time offer | Urgency, decisive action |
| **Narrow** | Single decision-maker, niche need | Precision approach, tailored value |
| **Precipitous** | High-risk, high-stakes deal | Heavy diligence, risk mitigation |

## 📝 Email Templates

### Competitive Positioning (Win Without Fighting)
```
Hi [Name],

I know you're evaluating several solutions. Let me save you some time.

Instead of comparing feature lists, here are three things our current clients tell us they couldn't find anywhere else:

1. [Unique differentiator 1]
2. [Unique differentiator 2]
3. [Unique differentiator 3]

If those aren't important to you, [Competitor X] might be a better fit — and I'm happy to say so.

But if they are, I'd love 20 minutes to show you how we deliver them.

Best,
[Your name]
```

### "Know Your Enemy" — Discovery Email
```
Subject: A quick question about [their situation]

Hi [Name],

I've been studying [their industry / company situation] and I noticed you're probably dealing with [specific challenge they face].

[Competitor / alternative] handles it this way: [their approach].

Here's our approach: [your differentiator].

What's your experience been with this so far?
```

### Urgency Without Aggression
```
Subject: What changes if we wait?

[Name],

Fair warning — I'm going to be direct.

[Problem they face] isn't getting easier. Every [week/month] you wait, it costs you approximately [X in lost revenue / Y in frustration].

I'm not saying you need to decide today. But if nothing changes in the next [quarter], what's the cost of staying where you are?

Worth discussing?
```

## ⚠️ Pitfalls

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---------|-------------|
| Over-attacking competitors | Makes you look desperate. Let the prospect draw their own conclusions |
| Prolonged negotiation | The longer it drags, the more likely the incumbent or status quo wins |
| Fighting on their ground | Don't compete on features or price where you're weak — reframe the battlefield |
| Showing all your cards | Reveal value incrementally; let them discover more as they engage |
| Mistaking preparation for action | Intelligence without execution is just research — ship |

---

## 🎯 When to Use

**Trigger conditions:**
- Competitive deals where you're going head-to-head
- Strategic account planning
- Long-term client relationship building
- Market entry or new territory expansion
- High-stakes proposals with multiple alternatives
- Any negotiation where you assess the other side's position

**Do NOT use when:**
- Quick transactional close needed (complicates simple deals)
- You lack competitive intelligence (guessing = losing)
- Relationship is collaborative, not adversarial (switch to Voss)
